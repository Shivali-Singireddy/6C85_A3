Reading & Critique

Insights about the data/visualization:

  • The number of new COVID-19 cases follows a cyclical pattern, with noticeable peaks and troughs. For instance, the cases steadily rose until mid-January 2021, then dropped to near zero by mid-June. This was followed by another surge in September, followed by a slight decline. The most significant peak occurred in January 2022, marking the highest number of new cases observed during the entire period.
  • Overall, outside of the cycles, there is an overarching upward trend in the number of new cases. The graph shows that each new year starts at the top of the cycle, and we can observe a significant rise in cases from January 2020 to January 2021. The same trend is evident from January 2021 to January 2022, indicating a year-over-year increase in new infections.
  • The data also suggests a clear seasonal pattern, with case numbers peaking during the winter months, particularly in January. Conversely, summer months, especially June, tend to see a decrease in cases, highlighting a possible seasonal variation in virus transmission. This might mean that the transmitablity of the virus is affected by the weather.
  • The data highlights a concerning trend in January 2022, where new cases surged to unprecedented levels, far surpassing previous years' trends. This sharp increase suggests that the virus may be becoming more infectious, which could raise significant public health concerns moving forward.

Design Critique:

    The graph effectively highlights year-to-year variations in new COVID-19 cases, making it easy to identify seasonal patterns and the cyclical nature of the virus. Specifically, it’s clear that summer months typically experience fewer cases, while winter months, particularly January, see a significant rise in infections. In general, this graph differs from the standard graphs typically found in research papers, which makes it challenging to pinpoint exact numbers. However, the trade-off is that this format effectively highlights the seasonality, making it much easier to spot seasonal trends at a glance. The use of dotted grey lines marking the months further increases the clarity of the graph by reducing the cognitive load needed to track the timeline. This makes it easier for the viewer to follow the progression of cases month by month. The graph also does not seem to have much clutter in it, which contributes to its simplicity and makes it easier to read. Adding more elements could disrupt this clarity, potentially overwhelming the audience and making it harder for them to know where to focus.
    However, while the graph does a good job illustrating overall trends, it could better emphasize the article’s key point of Omicron’s increased transmissibility. The visualization seems to highlight the seasonality and cyclical nature of cases more, which might cause readers to originally miss the huge increase in new cases because of Omicron. Adding labels to draw attention to the dramatic increase in cases from January 2021 to 2022, or highlighting this in the graph with annotations, would make the message clearer. Since the intended audience is the general public, anyone who could potentially be affected by the virus, it’s crucial that the graph immediately communicates the key takeaway. Additionally, the graph leaves some ambiguity regarding the exact number of cases in each month. While a legend shows a range of 0 to 150K, it’s unclear whether this represents the maximum number of cases or the size of the shaded area. Including more specific labels at key points and changing the legend to better represent the case numbers based on area in the graph would make the data easier to interpret and more informative for the viewer.

Final Visualization Design

Design Process & Design Decisions

There are two aspects of the data that I am most effectively trying to communicate. The first is that the Omicron variant is the significantly more infectious than previous COVID-19 variants. The second is that while it is more infectious, it is less fatal. These were the two main points in the article, which is why I wanted both them to be highlighted in the visualization in front of the article. The top half of the visualization effectively communicates the first part of the message, while the bottom half of visualization effectively communicates the second part of the main idea. The reason I chose to include new deaths to new cases ratio is because a huge number of deaths might be a result of a huge number of new cases. Therefore, to understand how fatal the disease actually is, we should compare how many people it kills to the number of people it has infected. While it might kill a decent number of people, if it infects a lot more people than it kills, it is overall less fatal than previous variants. I chose to use the area plot with a line because line graphs are familiar to most people, making them easier to interpret. I chose an area plot instead of a simple line graph to visually represent the total impact of the data, as the area beneath the line reflects the cumulative number of new cases and the ratio, indicating how many people were affected at each point. To further emphasize this, I shaded the area. For color choices, I used red to represent the number of new cases and black for the deaths-to-new-cases ratio. Red is commonly associated with alerts or something requiring attention, which aligns with the urgency of new COVID-19 cases, while black is often linked to death, making it a fitting choice for the ratio of fatalities. The visualization does effectively show the main points of the article by including labels with arrows to visually illustrate the main points. To further highlight the second point, I added a blue line representing the average death-to-new-cases ratio, allowing for a direct comparison with the current ratio. This helps to clearly demonstrate that the current ratio is significantly lower than it was previously. I chose blue for the line because it contrasts effectively with the black and ensures the line stands out visually. One aspect that this visualization fails to capture is the cyclic nature of this data and the seasonal variation. However, I did not think that this was the main point of the article, which is why I chose the type of graph I did for user interpretability in understanding the main points of the article.

Reflection

I believe that my final design does address some critiques surfaced in Step 2. It now does effectively emphasize the article’s main point, and even includes it in the title in case users are confused. The numbers are also much clearer now as there is y-axis legend whose lines are drawn across until the end of the x-axis. I actually really liked the “critique by redesign” approach because it effectively helped me identify challenges with communicating this data along with some design tensions and tradeoffs. Without taking time to analyze what worked well with the original graph and what didn’t I wouldn’t have been able to notice the pattern of seasonality in the original data, nor would I have accounted for it in potential redesigns. However, trying to understand why the graph designers chose this form of visualization helped bring this out to me, and made me more aware of the decisions I was making and what information I was losing. It also helped me see that as a designer, some patterns might seem obvious to me, but as a viewer, they might not be as obvious. This reminds me of the curse of knowledge, which is when you know that there is a pattern so your eyes are drawn to it, but your audience might not and as a result they might be confused by what you are trying highlight. To help avoid this, it can help to draw arrows or include some additional tool which can highlight the main point as well in case it is not immediately obvious to viewers.
